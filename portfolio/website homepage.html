What was the Holocaust  

 The holocaust refers to the systematic, state-sponsored genocide of European Jews and other ethnicities during the Second World War between 1941 and 1945, by Nazi Germany and its allies and collaborators. It was an attempt to destroy Jewish life itself: its long-established patterns of religious worship, ethics, culture, languages and livelihoods. In the end, six million Jews (two thirds of the entire Jewish population in Europe), and five million other ethnicities and victims died as the result of the Holocaust.

Why the Holocaust should be remembered 

 There are many reasons why the holocaust should be remembered, instead of just moving on and forgetting the past. The most valuable reason is that humanity must learn from its mistakes, to learn from history and to make sure that history does not repeat itself. Even though similar event though smaller in scale have occurred since. Another reason is to acknowledge that the Holocaust actually happened as there are many people who outright deny that it happened or downplay the scale of it.

 

 About this site

 This site was created by an ambassador for the Holocaust Educational Trust as part of a University project for media production. It aims to create awareness to key aspects of the Holocaust both during the Second World War, and the years leading up to it.
